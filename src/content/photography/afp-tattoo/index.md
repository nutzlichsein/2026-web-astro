Among the Dayak people of Borneo, flowers, leaves, animals, and bold black lines traced a journey through life. In the Mentawai Islands, long flowing patterns across the chest, shoulders, hands, and feet reflected a deep relationship with the natural world.<p><br><p>For decades, these traditions nearly disappeared. Missionaries, government pressure, urban migration, and the pull of modern life pushed many young people away from ancestral practices. But today, a new generation of tattoo artists is helping bring them back."

  - type: "text"
    content: "At a tattoo gathering in Maguwoharjo, Java, artists and enthusiasts came together to celebrate hand-tapping, a centuries-old method where a needle fixed to a stick is gently tapped into the skin by hand. Unlike modern tattoo machines, the process is slow, intimate, and deeply connected to ritual.<p><br><p>For artists like Herpianto Hendra, a Dayak tattooist, this revival is more than a trend. It is a way of reclaiming pride. More young Indonesians are now searching for tattoos that carry meaning—not just decoration, but memory, ancestry, and belonging."

  - type: "text"
    content: "The revival has also reached urban spaces. In Jakarta, a small museum dedicated to indigenous body art preserves photographs, tools, and stories from tattooed communities. There, artists continue to practice hand-tapping, adapting old techniques with sterilized needles and modern safety standards while keeping the spirit of the tradition alive.<p><br><p><i>'There is a growing trend now, there are more people who want to learn about traditional tattooing – I am very happy about it,'</i> said Herpianto Hendra, a tattoo artist who uses the ancient method and is a member of Borneo's Dayak tribespeople.<p><br><p> <i><b>'I am proud that my culture is being recognised.'</b></i>"
  - type: "text"
    content: "Still, the future is fragile. Some tattoo styles from remote communities remain at risk of being forgotten. Without careful documentation, this knowledge—passed through hands, skin, and memory—could disappear.<p><br><p>But for now, each tap of the needle becomes an act of remembrance. Each pattern becomes a bridge between past and present. And each tattooed body carries a story that refuses to fade."
